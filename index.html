What is Internal Auditing?

Internal auditing is an independent, objective assurance and consulting activity designed to add value and improve an organization's operations. It helps an organization accomplish its objectives by bringing a systematic, disciplined approach to evaluate and improve the effectiveness of risk management, control, and governance processes.

Internal vs. External Audit

Internal Audit

Reports to management & the board. Focuses on improving internal processes, controls, and risk management. Continuous activity.

External Audit

Reports to shareholders. Focuses on expressing an opinion on financial statements. Typically an annual activity.

The Role of Internal Audit in ISO Frameworks

Internal audits are a mandatory and critical component of ISO management systems, ensuring compliance, driving continual improvement, and verifying that the system is effective and achieving its intended outcomes.

ISO 9001:2015

Quality Management

Internal audits verify that the Quality Management System (QMS) conforms to both organizational requirements and the ISO 9001 standard. They assess process effectiveness and identify opportunities for improving product/service quality and customer satisfaction.

ISO/IEC 27001:2022

Information Security

Audits are essential for checking if the Information Security Management System (ISMS) is effectively implemented and maintained. Auditors assess the suitability of security controls, review risk assessments, and ensure the protection of information assets.

ISO/IEC 27701:2019

Privacy Information

As an extension to ISO 27001, internal audits for this standard focus on the Privacy Information Management System (PIMS). They ensure that the processing of Personally Identifiable Information (PII) complies with privacy regulations and stakeholder expectations.

Common Types of Internal Audits

Internal audit activities are diverse, addressing various aspects of an organization's operations and compliance landscape. The focus often depends on industry, risk profile, and strategic objectives.

Crafting the Audit Report: The 5 Cs

Criteria (What should be)

The standards, measures, or expectations used in making an evaluation (e.g., policies, procedures, laws, regulations).

Condition (What is)

The factual evidence that the internal auditor found in the course of the examination (the current state).

Cause (Why it happened)

The reason for the difference between the expected and actual conditions. This is the root cause of the finding.

Consequence (The effect)

The risk or exposure the organization encounters because the condition is not consistent with the criteria (the impact).

Corrective Action (What should be done)

The action recommendations provided by the auditor to correct the condition and prevent its recurrence.

Sample Audit Checklists by Industry

  • Inventory Management: Are inventory records accurate and is there a process for regular cycle counts?
  • Production Controls: Are production processes documented, followed, and monitored for efficiency and waste?
  • Quality Assurance: Is there a documented QA process, including inspection of raw materials and finished goods?
  • Health & Safety: Does the plant comply with all occupational health and safety regulations? Are safety drills conducted regularly?
  • Supply Chain: Are supplier contracts current and are supplier performances regularly reviewed?
  • Regulatory Compliance: Is the institution compliant with AML (Anti-Money Laundering) and KYC (Know Your Customer) regulations?
  • Loan Portfolio: Are loan origination processes followed, credit risks properly assessed, and documentation complete?
  • Data Security: Are customer data and sensitive financial information adequately protected against unauthorized access?
  • Capital Adequacy: Does the institution maintain capital reserves as required by regulatory bodies?
  • Fraud Detection: Are there effective controls in place to detect and prevent internal and external fraud?
  • Claims Processing: Are claims processed accurately, in a timely manner, and in accordance with policy terms?
  • Underwriting: Are underwriting guidelines documented and consistently applied? Are risks appropriately priced?
  • Reinsurance: Are reinsurance agreements properly documented and is counterparty risk assessed?
  • Regulatory Solvency: Does the company meet all statutory capital and surplus requirements?
  • Information Security: Is policyholder PII (Personally Identifiable Information) protected in compliance with privacy laws?
  • Cash Handling: Are point-of-sale (POS) reconciliation procedures followed daily? Are cash deposits secured?
  • Inventory & Shrinkage: Are controls in place to manage inventory and minimize shrinkage (theft, damage)?
  • PCI Compliance: Is the company compliant with Payment Card Industry Data Security Standards (PCI DSS) for all card transactions?
  • Supplier Management: Are vendor terms and pricing regularly reviewed and managed effectively?
  • Sales & Returns Policy: Is the returns process well-defined to prevent fraud, and are sales promotions properly authorized?
